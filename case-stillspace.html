Why This Product Exists

Most mental health apps are built around productivity: streaks, goals, daily habits. They work well for people with moderate stress. But for someone in a depressive episode — when getting out of bed is a victory — these apps feel like another thing to fail at.

I wanted to create something different: a companion that meets you where you are, with zero pressure and zero judgment. Not a therapy replacement, but a quiet place to land when you need one.

Design Philosophy

Every design decision starts from one question: "Would this feel safe for someone on their worst day?"

Core principles

  • Warmth first: Warm palette (peach, lavender, soft grays), rounded shapes, zero visual aggression. The interface should feel like a hug — never push, never rush
  • Gentle clarity: One screen, one action. Minimal cognitive load. Copy is caring, not clinical — it acknowledges struggle without judgment
  • Quiet delight: Subtle micro-interactions — a heartbeat haptic in breathing mode, soft confetti after completing a self-care action. Celebrations feel earned and intimate
  • No toxic positivity: The app never says "stay positive" or "you've got this." Instead: "Just being is already huge work. You're doing it."

App Flow

The core experience adapts to how the user feels right now — not a fixed routine, but a responsive companion.

Mood check-in → Adaptive actions → Journal

Three energy levels. No complex questionnaires. The app responds with micro-activities matched to your current state — from "place your hand on your chest" to "go outside, buy coffee."

Alternative paths

  • Just Be Together: For when you can't do anything — a breathing circle with heartbeat haptics and ambient sound. No instructions, no timers. Just presence.
  • Crisis support: Verified helpline numbers always one tap away — contextualized by language. Direct access to professional psychological support.
  • Quick wins: Record small victories anytime — opened a window, drank water. That counts.

Building with AI

Still Space was designed and developed with Claude (Anthropic) as a creative and engineering partner. This wasn't about replacing design thinking — it was about amplifying it.

What AI enabled

  • Rapid prototyping: From concept sketches to working SwiftUI code in hours, not weeks
  • Content creation at scale: ~150 localized self-care tasks across 3 energy levels and multiple languages, each carefully crafted for tone and clinical appropriateness
  • Design system as code: Color tokens, typography, spacing — defined once in design, immediately reflected in production code
  • Iteration speed: Testing ideas in real code instead of static mockups — seeing how animations feel, how copy reads in context, how flows connect

What stayed human

  • Product vision: The "why" — understanding what people in crisis actually need, not what apps typically offer
  • Emotional design: Tone of voice, visual warmth, the feeling of safety — these require empathy AI can support but not originate
  • Ethical decisions: Privacy-first architecture, crisis resource verification, no gamification of suffering
  • Curation: AI generates options; a designer decides what serves the user

Under the Hood

  • SwiftUI — native iOS, zero external dependencies
  • 100% offline-first — optional iCloud sync for backup
  • Zero data collection — no accounts, no analytics, no ads
  • Multilingual — Ukrainian and English
  • Accessibility — WCAG AA contrast, Dynamic Type, VoiceOver, minimum 44pt touch targets
  • Dark & light themes — warm palette in both modes
